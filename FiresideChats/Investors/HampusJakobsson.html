Pale Blue Dot VC: Hampus Jakobsson


In this edited conversation with Hampus Jakobsson, the co founder of Pale Blue DOt, Peter Green spoke about xy,c books and dreams

How has Pale Blue Dot VC evolved to where it is now?



Pale Blue Dot is 16 months old now and run by three general partners. I met Heidi when I was advising her previous start-up, and I initially met Joel after angel investing in one of his companies.

In terms of my journey into climate-tech investing, I'm originally a computer scientist and come from a deep-tech family where thinking big, abstract thoughts was the norm. When I was 20, I stumbled into building an art exhibition by mistake. This evolved into a business which attracted plenty of attention, including from a Sony Ericsson director. This director had been so impressed by our venue that they invited us to create a PlayStation phone which Sony was demanding. We weren't initially interested, but the director was persistent and offered $50,000 per phone model, equal to the biggest amount we’d previously been paid by our art venues. Sensing the opportunity, we decided to pivot away from art exhibitions, and built a company from six friends to 180 people with offices in Taipei, Tokyo, San Francisco and Chicago.

We were acquired by Blackberry after this. Upon the acquisition, the CEO inquired whether I wanted to run M&A for the company. Looking back, this was a great lesson for me because it taught me about the role of finance. As a kid, I'd believed that science changed the world. As a result, I'd lived 15 years of computer science, entrepreneurship, and building. During those two years at Blackberry it became clear that this was not enough. I realised that finance ultimately changes the world. In order to make a difference, you needed a combination of depth, entrepreneurship and finance. So, that’s when I started angel investing. In those days, I invested in things that very few others paid attention to, from fintech, to mental health start-ups and remote work. The stuff that nobody was touching in 2010. I invested in 100+ companies.

I then took the opportunity to join a deep tech fund in Berlin. That’s where I started looking at more climate companies because that's what I wanted to do. Climate's particularly interesting because it remains a very gnarly topic. You need everything; depth, science, entrepreneurship, and action-orientation. I made a number of deals there but realised how hard it was to change the venture model to suit the needs of climate companies. As a result, I decided to leave and eventually created Pale Blue Dot with Heidi and Joel. We opened the fund in June last year and have done 14 investments so far.


We created Pale Blue Dot for a number of reasons. Firstly, it's frustrating that there aren't enough operator- led climate-tech funds who know how to be in the trenches with early stage founders. The main reason is it's simply a nasty stage to be in. You have to do a lot of stupid bets. A major challenge relates to assessing founders. Do they really want to change the world for the better? Will they stay the course or will they sell their technology to the military? I jokingly say that it's a combo of Texas Hold'em and reading papers. The technical stuff is searchable, but picking people is more of a bet. As a team, we’d previously done 150 odd investments in start-ups and people. As a result, we believed that we might be well placed to pick the right people.



Given your previous investments in algae-tech, whats you’re opinion on the space?




In general, the biggest problem with algae-tech relates to demand. How can you create something which has a natural demand that people will pay for? At the end of the day (part-joke, part-truth), if you're selling to a company, they care about three things, and if you're selling to a person, they care about three other things. Companies care about increasing revenue, decreasing cost or decreasing risk. Therefore, when you start talking about increasing biodiversity, you're not checking one of those boxes. On the other hand, if you're selling to consumers, they think about getting paid, made or laid. This is similar to what Dave McClure said many years ago. Therefore, are you going to tick one of those boxes with an algae product? You need to nail one of the following components; reduced price, increased convenience or better quality. Right now, algae doesn't really check any of those box. That’s a problem.

Nevertheless, there are some exceptions to the rule. For example, the company betta fish positioned it's algae product as an alternative to tuna, but 30% cheaper. Using algae instead of tuna comes with additional soft benefits, like increasing biodiversity, creating local jobs, and providing consumers with healthy food. However, the key component is that algae is much cheaper than tuna. So, they highlighted these benefits and the buyers said "wow, you've made a better tuna." This is great product placement. When it's described as better tuna, people don’t care that it’s algae. As a result, the companies you sell to won't talk about price in their marketing language, they’ll say that they now have an alternative to tuna which is healthier for you, and healthier for the planet. In conclusion, if you can increase the margins for companies, decrease the price for the consumer and it’s better for the world, you’ve really nailed it.



What problem areas in the climate conversation do you really hope to see innovations and solutions?




There are a number of areas but we are thinking particularly hard about biodiversity, moving pension fund money and improving supply chains.

I'm super interested in biodiversity. I think we're going to wake up one day and realise that we are all focused on carbon when the problem is biodiversity. We're all talking about carbon, but we don't talk about the thing that creates it. When we fixate on carbon that way, then carbon offsetting works and planting eucalyptus trees works, but these solutions can be terrible for biodiversity. Ecosystems are really complex. They need a predator-prey system, they need different hierarchies, and different things keeps everything in check. I fear that we're building a system where we are actually promoting the destruction of biodiversity in the name of climate.

Another area of interest revolves around moving pension fund money. Pale Blue Dot is a $100 million fund. As a result, our biggest job involves picking the right people. When it comes down to investing sufficient funds into these teams, we're simply not enough by ourselves. As a result, in order to figure out climate change, we need to move pension fund money into these ventures. It’s ironic that people are saving for a rainy day in these pension funds. What these funds aren't telling you is that those rainy day's will be class 5 Hurricanes. We need to continue thinking about moving big finance and that's complicated.

In addition, we're also very interested in making supply chains shorter and more transparent. Improving this area can have massive benefits for communities and climate. Generally speaking, capitalism has created longer and longer, more opaque supply chains. It's so clear that there's something fundamentally broken here. For climate reasons, it's dangerous because we don't know where goods are coming from. For example, we don't know how much water some products consume and we don't know whether other objects are recycled.

The system we have designed likes and promotes cheap labour, and separates production from consumption as well as it can. Currently, price is the only thing that the market is worried about. Despite this, we've seen massive price spikes in 2020. That just shows how we cannot handle or control anything, including the only thing we care about. What happens when you look at other variables in the supply chain? What about things which damage the environment? How much worse off are these externalities than price for the planet? There's a huge need to create transparency so we can really understand what's going on.
