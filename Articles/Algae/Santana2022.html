Get out of my ocean.


How can we get rid of invasive seaweed species? That's a challenge taken on by Santana et al, a team from Spain hoping to rid the mediterranean of the invasive pacific seaweed Rugulopteryx okamurae. Let's take a quick look at their epic piece from 2022, which highlights their bioplastic production process.







Summary




Recognise this?


Essentially, after freeze drying and milling the seaweed, it is thoroughly mixed with glycerol (a plasticiser used to improve flexibility and viability of the bioplastic) to obtain a homogeneous blend that was eventually used for injection molding at a mold temperature of 90, 120 and 150 ◦C.


If you were wondering what injection moulding looked like:




So… How did it fare? Pretty well it seems. It showed typical thermoplastic behavior and had some decent mechanical properties. The higher the content of seaweed, the greater the viscoelastic properties, rigidity and resistance the bioplastics display. Thus, the sample with RO/Gly ratio of 70/30 displayed the higher values of those properties when compared to 50/50 or 60/40 ratios. The team does state these should be improved in order to reach the same properties as already available plastics. They suggest creeating composites with other biodegradable polymers and changing the processing properties as examples.


It's an interesting paper, and I'm impressed by the rigour of their investigation. It's a highly detailed process report, and a thorough breakdown of their process provides ample opportunity to dive deep into their thought process. In terms of relevance, it's fantastic. . It's great to see more teams working on these kind of technologies and we're seeing more and more governments seeking to remove invasive species. What better way to do this than use it for bioplastics?
